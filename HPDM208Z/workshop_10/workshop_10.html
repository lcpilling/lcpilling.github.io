2 Pharmacogenetics (PGx), statins, and SLCO1B1

Drug efficacy and side effects varies in patients prescribed medications. Reasons can include co-morbidities and patient characteristics. Genetic variants are known to influence efficacy and safety of some medicines. PGx is the study of how genetic variation affects individual response to drugs.

Statins are prescribed to lower cholesterol levels for primary prevention of cardiovascular disease (CVD). Atorvastatin is the most common in the UK.

Statins act by reducing cholesterol synthesis in the liver. Proteins that transport statins to their site of action include OATP1B1. This is encoded by the gene SLCO1B1.

A common genetic variant rs4149056 T>C in the SLCO1B1 gene results in an amino acid substitution (p.Val174Ala) meaning the protein structure is altered. We will use the shorthand SLCO1B1*5 to refer to this variant, which is reported to impair transport and reduce statin effectiveness.

In this workshop you will determine whether SLCO1B1*5 (encoded by rs4149056 genotype) impacts statin efficacy.

The ClinPGx website contains lots of useful pharmacogenetics information. Have a look at rs4149056 (www.clinpgx.org/variant/PA166154579). This summarizes evidence for drugs affected, how common the variant is, and other information.

7 Pharmacogenetic implications

7.1 Biological mechanism

The SLCO1B1*5 variant (rs4149056 C allele) results in reduced function of the OATP1B1 transporter protein. This protein is responsible for transporting statins from the blood into liver cells, where statins inhibit cholesterol synthesis.

Key pharmacogenetic findings:

  1. No effect in untreated individuals: The variant does not affect baseline LDL cholesterol in people not taking statins, as the transporter is not involved in endogenous cholesterol regulation.

  2. Reduced efficacy in treated individuals: Carriers of the SLCO1B1*5 variant (especially CC homozygotes) have impaired statin transport into liver cells, resulting in:

    • Reduced drug efficacy (higher LDL despite treatment)
    • Potentially increased risk of statin-related side effects due to higher systemic drug concentrations

  3. Gene-drug interaction: This is a classic example of pharmacogenetics—genetic variation affecting drug response. The variant only matters when the drug is present.

7.2 Clinical relevance

Atorvastatin is one of the most commonly prescribed medications worldwide for cardiovascular disease prevention. Understanding who will respond well to the medication (and who may need alternative treatments or higher doses) is clinically important for:

  • Personalized treatment selection: Identify patients who may benefit from alternative statins or non-statin lipid-lowering therapies
  • Dose optimization: CC homozygotes may require higher doses to achieve target LDL levels
  • Side effect prediction: Reduced hepatic uptake may increase risk of muscle-related side effects

8.3 Is this clinically significant?

While the relative risk may be elevated (OR > 1), we must consider:

  1. Absolute numbers: How many SLCO1B1*5 homozygotes actually have high LDL?
    • Even if OR = 1.5, if 10% more CC individuals have high LDL, this means the excess uncontrolled LDL whilst prescribed atorvastatin could be considered modest
  2. Positive predictive value (PPV): What proportion of those carrying two risk alleles actually have the adverse outcome?
    • We will consider this further below - but low values may mean false positives if tested clinically
  3. Allele frequency: The CC genotype is rare (~2.5% in this European population)
    • This limits the population-level impact and utility of routine screening

Interpretation of predictive utility:

  • Low sensitivity: The genotype test will miss most people who have high LDL. The test is missing nearly 95% of the people with high LDL. Majority of “high LDL” not due to *5 genotype.
  • High specificity: Most people without the risk genotype do have normal LDL
  • Low PPV: At 61.0%, if you receive a positive result, there is a 61% probability you have the condition and a 39% probability it is a “false positive.”
  • Low NPV: A 48.0% NPV is very low (worse than a coin flip). It means that if you get a negative result, there is only a 48% chance you are actually disease-free

Clinical conclusion: While SLCO1B1*5 genotyping identifies a subset at higher risk of poor response, its low PPV and rarity mean it has limited utility as a pre-treatment screening tool. Current clinical practice of:

  1. Prescribing atorvastatin to all suitable patients
  2. Monitoring LDL levels post-treatment
  3. Adjusting therapy (dose increase or alternative agent) based on measured LDL response

…remains more practical and cost-effective than pre-treatment genotyping for most patients.

However, genotyping may still be valuable for:

  • Research to understand mechanisms of drug response variability
  • Patients with unexplained poor response or side effects
  • Future multi-gene pharmacogenetic panels that combine multiple variants

9 Discussion: Broader context

9.1 Polygenic risk scores vs. single variants

In previous weeks you learned about polygenic risk scores (PRS) that combine information from hundreds or thousands of genetic variants to predict disease risk. In contrast, today you analyzed a single pharmacogenetic variant.

Key differences:

  • Pharmacogenetics: Often involves variants in genes directly involved in drug metabolism or transport. Single variants can have large, clinically relevant effects.
  • Disease risk prediction: Usually involves many variants with small effects. PRS aggregate these to improve prediction.

For drug response, both approaches may be relevant:

  • Single high-impact variants (like SLCO1B1*5) for specific drug-gene pairs
  • Polygenic scores for complex drug responses involving multiple pathways

9.2 Stratified medicine in practice

This workshop demonstrates a core principle of stratified medicine: identifying patient subgroups who will respond differently to treatment. Genetic biomarkers like SLCO1B1*5 can:

  1. Guide treatment selection
  2. Predict treatment response
  3. Identify patients at risk of adverse effects
  4. Enable more efficient clinical trials (enrichment strategies)

However, implementation requires evidence that:

  • Genetic testing improves clinical outcomes
  • Benefits outweigh costs
  • Results change clinical decision-making

9.3 Future directions

Pharmacogenomics is an expanding field. Resources like:

  • CPIC Guidelines (Clinical Pharmacogenetics Implementation Consortium): Evidence-based gene-drug clinical practice guidelines
  • ClinPGx (www.clinpgx.org): Comprehensive pharmacogenomics knowledge base

…provide up-to-date information on clinically actionable pharmacogenetic variants across many therapeutic areas.
